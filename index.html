The GIS Community of Practice is an important tool for GIS staff to build professional relationships, to learn from and leverage other’s areas of expertise, and to stay informed about what is new in terms of best practices, efficiencies, software, and government infrastructure.

Our theme, Here, There, and Everywhere, celebrates our community - who we are, where we’re headed, and the impact of our work. We are Here together to foster connections, learn and inspire each other; There meeting the needs of our clients using the best GIS tools and techniques; and Everywhere serving all sectors across all parts of the province.

Our sub-theme, Harnessing the Power of Place…Together, references the 2021 Geospatial Strategy (titled Harnessing the Power of Place), the first sector wide plan in more than a decade. The strategy casts a vision for equipping government to face today’s challenges through geospatial data-driven decision making. Our community is integral to the success of this vision and our meeting Together is an important step towards implementation as we share our expertise and build connections.

Our hope is that the GIS CoP members and our infrastructure partners in attendance will recognize the breadth and importance of our work supporting government priorities and build a supportive network of colleagues to draw upon for finding solutions for clients.

Space, Place, and Meaning: autonomy, ethics and sharing in the age of standardization.

Space, Place, and Meaning:
autonomy, ethics and sharing in the age of standardization

Presenters: Peter L. Pulsifer, Ph.D. (Carleton University)

In all domains of society (social, economic, research etc.), the volume, variety and velocity of data continues to increase rapidly. Big Data, Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning are terms that have become part of the mainstream lexicon. The move towards interconnected, interoperable information systems that support the resulting need for enhanced data flows is driving the development and adoption of information technology standards.

Projects such as the Canadian Consortium for Arctic Data Interoperability are using standards combined with interoperable services to make data from and about the Arctic more available. However, effective and ethical implementation of standards requires that we examine the process through a critical lens by identifying the broader implications of standardization and questioning foundational conceptualizations like “space” and “place”. As a non-Indigenous practitioner, the author shares observations on the application of standardization and interoperable systems as a partner in the coproduction of Indigenous information systems.
